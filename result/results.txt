EXPERIMENT SETUP
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Dataset           : Mini-ImageNet (100 classes)
Training images   : 50,000 (500 per class)
Testing images    : 10,000 (100 per class)
Input resolution  : 96 x 96
Random seed       : 42

Compression method: 2D Discrete Wavelet Transform
Wavelet           : Haar
Decomposition     : 2 levels
Thresholding      : Top-K hard thresholding (per RGB channel)
Compression ratios: 2:1, 5:1, 10:1

================================================================================
  KEY OBSERVATIONS
================================================================================

1. Mild compression (2:1) is essentially free.
   Both models lose less than 1 percentage point of accuracy. Keeping 50 %
   of the wavelet coefficients preserves enough information that the
   classifiers are effectively unaffected. This is consistent with the
   energy-compaction property of the wavelet transform.

2. Moderate compression (5:1) begins to bite.
   Accuracy drops by 4-6 pp. MobileNetV2 degrades faster than ResNet-18
   here (-6.03 pp vs -4.14 pp), despite having a higher baseline. This
   suggests that the depth-wise-separable convolutions in MobileNetV2
   rely more heavily on mid-frequency texture cues that hard thresholding
   removes early.

3. Aggressive compression (10:1) is catastrophic.
   Both models lose roughly a quarter of their accuracy in absolute terms
   (ResNet-18: -23.30 pp, MobileNetV2: -27.30 pp). Keeping only 10 % of
   wavelet coefficients removes enough high- and mid-frequency content
   that many class-discriminative features disappear.

4. The degradation curve is highly non-linear.
   Moving from 2:1 to 5:1 costs a few percentage points; moving from 5:1
   to 10:1 costs roughly five times as much. The dominant failure regime
   sits between these two operating points.

5. MobileNetV2 is less robust than ResNet-18.
   Although MobileNetV2 wins on the original test set, it loses that lead
   at 5:1 and falls below ResNet-18 at 10:1. For deployment scenarios
   where input images are likely to be heavily compressed, ResNet-18
   appears to be the safer choice on this dataset.


================================================================================
  PER-CLASS SENSITIVITY (10:1 COMPRESSION)
================================================================================

The per-class analysis identifies the top 10 classes with the largest
accuracy drop between the original and 10:1 compressed test sets.

Visual inspection of sample images from these classes suggests that
fine textures, repetitive patterns, and subtle inter-class features are
the most fragile - exactly the content that lives in the high-frequency
wavelet sub-bands that hard thresholding discards first.

(Specific class IDs and drop values are produced by the notebook at
runtime and printed in the "Most affected classes" output block.)


================================================================================
  CONCLUSION
================================================================================

Wavelet-based lossy image compression has a non-uniform effect on CNN
classification accuracy:

  - Compression ratios up to 2:1 are safe for both models tested.
  - 5:1 compression causes a measurable but recoverable drop (4-6 pp).
  - 10:1 compression causes severe degradation (>20 pp), making it
    unsuitable for deployment without compression-aware training.

Across architectures, ResNet-18 is more robust to aggressive wavelet
compression than MobileNetV2, even though MobileNetV2 has the higher
uncompressed baseline. The choice of architecture should therefore
account for the expected compression regime at inference time.
